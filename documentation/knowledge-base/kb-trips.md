# Trips & travel

*Knowledge base article · seeded 2026-07-19 · covers trip capture,
framing, one-way journeys, stops, and the trips-vs-moves distinction.*

## What a trip is

A trip is a journey with shape: an origin, an ordered itinerary, a
destination that marked the turn toward home, and (usually) the way
back. It layers *over* your pins — the origin, destination, and stops
are all ordinary globe pins — and it carries its own recollections and
jots, separate from any one place's.

Trips never touch your residential sequence. Framing a trip cannot
reorder your homes, and deleting one never deletes a pin.

## Two ways to start one

**Destination-first (the main flow).** Drop a pin where the journey
turned toward home → choose **Trip** → pick a subtype (Vacation /
Professional / Road trip) → save. That alone is a valid saved trip — a
*draft*, wearing a "needs framing" badge until you give it an origin.
Drop destination pins freely as a travel skeleton and frame them
whenever.

**From the home it started at.** Select a home → its trip strip →
**"Start a trip from here."** A banner asks you to pin where the journey
went; the pin form opens pre-set to Trip, and the framing step arrives
with that home pre-selected as origin.

## Framing: the questions and what they mean

- **Where did the trip start?** — the origin. Suggested for you (the
  armed home, the destination's own home-era, or your Home Base);
  "Decide later" keeps it a draft. "＋ Pin a new origin on the globe…"
  exists for a home that predates your sequence — it saves as a
  not-yet-placed home.
- **Returned to the origin (round trip)** — checked by default, because
  most trips loop home. **Uncheck it for a one-way journey** (a
  relocation drive, a departure): the globe then draws only the outward
  route, and the Travel Journal won't show an "and back."
- **Title / When / Year** — the when-phrase is your words, verbatim. The
  Year box orders your Travel Journal; only what you *type* is used —
  the chronicle never guesses a year from your phrase. No year yet? The
  trip files under "Sometime."

## "Which home was I living in?" vs. "Where did the trip start?"

The pin form asks the first; the framing step asks the second. They
sound alike but mean different things:

- The **anchor** ties the destination *place* to an era of your life
  ("this belongs to the chalet chapter").
- The **origin** is where this particular *journey* began.

They usually have the same answer — and when you start a trip from a
home, both default to it — but they can differ: a conference city might
anchor to your Boston years while one particular trip there started from
your summer place.

## Stops, legs, and routes

With a trip selected on the globe, **Route** enters route-building:
clicking pins (or dropping new ones) appends stops in travel order.
Stops before the destination are the way out; stops after it are the way
back — the destination is the turnaround. Each stop is a real pin with
its own recollection if it deserves one. Trip routes stay hidden behind
the **Trips** toggle so your home thread stays dominant; a selected trip
always shows its full route.

## Reframing history

- **An existing pin was really a trip.** Its detail card offers *"This
  was a journey? Frame it as a trip"* — the pin keeps its identity,
  anchor line, recollections, and jots; it simply gains trip shape.
- **Unframe** removes the trip and restores the pin exactly as it was.
- **Repeat visits** to the same place are separate trips on the same
  pin — "Another trip here" starts the next one. Each keeps its own
  dates, purpose, and stories.
- **Home Base**: designate the home most trips leave from, and every new
  trip suggests it as origin automatically.

## A trip or a move?

A one-way journey that *ends where you then lived* is both — and the
chronicle keeps the two truths in their right places:

- The **move** belongs to the spine: the new home's pin, its slot in
  your sequence, and the reason you moved.
- The **journey itself** — the cross-country drive, the stops, the
  stories — belongs to a one-way trip.

Record both, and neither steps on the other. The drive's narrative lives
on the trip ([see Recollections, jots & write-ups](kb-recollections-and-jots.md));
the arrival lives in the new home's own story.
